Japan — November Roy & Family · Two Weeks

Week 1 Tokyo · Roy solo

Suica Card — Get one each on arrival. Works on all trains, buses, and even convenience stores. Load money onto it and tap on/off everything.

Shinkansen Pass — No longer worth it for most tourists. Only covers JR lines — privately owned lines are not included, and there are a lot of them. Only consider it if you are on a shinkansen literally every single day.

Google Maps — Works excellently for train navigation in Japan. Use it for everything.

Airport Limousine Bus — Highly recommended over trains or taxis. Handles luggage properly — no lugging bags through peak hour trains. Much better for elderly parents or anyone with multiple bags. Runs direct routes between airports and major city hotels.

Avoid trains at peak hour with luggage — it's a nightmare.

Avoid taxis for groups — limited on how many people and bags fit, and expensive.

Bounce Luggage — On your last day after checkout, store bags at a local partner business, pay per piece, collect later. Explore hands-free. usebounce.com

Takkyubin (luggage forwarding) — Send bags between cities ahead of you. Drop off at hotel front desk or any convenience store (Lawson, FamilyMart, 7-Eleven). Have your receiving address ready in Japanese. Arrives next day — plan ahead.

eSIM — Get one via Klook before you leave. Search "Japan eSIM". Activate it at home so you are connected the moment you land. No hunting for a SIM at the airport.

Japan is still very cash-heavy — smaller restaurants, shrines, local spots, and vending machines are often cash only. Never assume card is accepted.

Get yen at the airport or 7-Eleven ATMs — most reliable for foreign cards. Keep cash on you at all times.

Check your debit card for foreign transaction fees. Some cards (e.g. Ubank) have zero fees — use these wherever card is accepted.

When paying by card, always select pay in JPY (not AUD). The merchant's forex rate is almost always worse than your bank's rate. Applies to every card transaction.
